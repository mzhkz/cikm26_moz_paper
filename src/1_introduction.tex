\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
Large language model (LLM) applications such as GPT~\cite{chatgpt}, Gemini~\cite{gemini}, and Claude~\cite{claude} increasingly incorporate web search functions, providing internet users with new ways of accessing information~\cite{webgpt-arxiv21, geo-apmr+24, zhai2024llm-and-future-of-information-retrieval}. 
Information retrieval has become a primary use case for LLMs, with a survey showing that about $30\%$ of all prompts involve information seeking~\cite{how-people-use-chatgpt-nber25}. 
Systems that perform web searches and generate answers are known as \emph{generative search engines} (GSEs)~\cite{geo-apmr+24}. 
Web search services are rapidly transitioning from traditional search engines to GSE-based systems~\cite{openai-chatgpt-search24, google-gemini-search24, perplexity-comet25, dia-browser25, microsoft-bing-chat23, anthropic-multiagent25}, changing the way users access and interpret web content.

% GSEs not only alter the delivery of information but also reshape its nature. 
% Whereas conventional search engines direct users to primary information sources such as official documents, GSEs provide synthesized answers that summarize, interpret, and personalize web content. 
% LLMs also exhibit bias~\cite{bias-and-fairness-in-large-language-models-cl24, on-the-dangers-of-stochastic-parrots-facc21, holisticevaluationlanguagemodels-arxiv22, bbc-ai-search-engines-2024}, meaning that users receive secondary or higher-order information produced by LLMs~\cite{the-spread-of-true-and-false-news-online-science2018}.
% This transformation enhances accessibility but centralizes the selection and interpretation of web content in GSEs; moreover, major GSEs are black-box systems, introducing vulnerabilities such as answer inaccuracies.

GSEs cite content published on the web by various publishers, including attackers with malicious or profit-seeking intent; therefore, they remain vulnerable to poisoning attacks that inject malicious content into the web and produce attacker-intended answers~\cite{poisonedrag-arxiv24, poisoning-web-scale-training-datasets-is-practical-sp24}. 
When GSEs generate answers lacking factuality, the cause is not only LLM hallucinations~\cite{sirens-song-in-the-ai-ocean-coli25, survey-of-hallucination-in-natural-language-generation-acmcsurv23} but also the lack of factuality in the web content obtained through web searches~\cite{retrieval-helps-or-hurts-deeper-arxiv24, poisonedrag-arxiv24, a-comprehensive-survey-on-trustworthy-recommender-systems-arxiv22}.
Web content can be published and modified by any user with any intent~\cite{poisoning-web-scale-training-datasets-is-practical-sp24, poisonedrag-arxiv24}.
Prior studies formalize poisoning attacks that place disinformation and misinformation on the web to generate attacker-intended answers in systems that retrieve from external databases and generate answers, including GSEs, because these systems can cite such content and generate text that reflects it~\cite{poisoning-web-scale-training-datasets-is-practical-sp24, poisonedrag-arxiv24, yu2025safetydevolutionaiagents}.
Specific to GSEs, some studies present patterns in the styles of web content that GSEs prefer to cite in answers and automatically optimize contents for citation exposure~\cite{geo-apmr+24, wu2025generativesearchengineslikeai}.
Although they are beneficial for primary information providers that seek to increase exposure of their web content as citations, they can also facilitate poisoning attacks.

Existing evaluation criteria aim to assess how faithfully the cited content is reflected in the answers generated by retrieval-and-generation systems, as well as the extent to which cited content dominates the generated answers.
Several studies evaluate faithfulness by measuring the textual and semantic consistency between the cited web content and the generated answers~\cite{citeeval-acl25, ragas-eacl24, ares-naacl24, evaluating-verifiability-in-generative-search-engines-emnlp23, enabling-llms-to-generate-text-with-citations-emnlp23, ranking-generated-summaries-by-correctness-acl2019, evaluating-the-factual-consistency-of-abstractive-text-summarization-emnlp20, alignscore-acl23, feqa-acl20}.
In GEO, a major evaluation criterion is the domination score, which represents the proportion of cited text relative to the total text volume of the generated answer~\cite{geo-apmr+24, wu2025generativesearchengineslikeai}.
However, these studies alone cannot capture the attack surface of GSEs for PoisonedRAG attacks.
This is because these studies target systems called \emph{RAG systems}, which retrieve from curated, fixed, and closed external authoritative databases to generate answers.
In contrast, GSEs treat the web as an external database that is open to anyone and dynamically updated, allowing attackers to easily inject malicious content that GSEs can cite.
Due to these differences, evaluation criteria proposed for \emph{RAG systems} cannot adequately assess the reliability and safety of GSEs.

Moreover, existing citation evaluation also does not consider the impact of personalization on citation behavior.
LLM applications such as ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini actively personalize inference to improve user experience.
To reveal the attack surface of GSEs, we need to consider the effects of personalization on their citation behavior; however, how personalization influences the selection of citations in GSEs remains insufficiently explored.

To address this gap, we introduce a novel framework to characterize the attack surface of GSEs for PoisonedRAG attacks in the political domain.
Our framework poses political questions under user profiles that vary the user's level of prior political knowledge and ideology, enabling systematic analysis of how personalization influences citation behavior.
Each citation is classified by its \emph{content-injection barrier}, categorized as low, medium, or high, based on the practical difficulty of publishing content on that source, as determined through LLM-as-a-Judge~\cite{survey-llm-as-a-judge-arxiv24, judging-llm-as-a-judge-with-mt-bench-and-chatbot-arena-arxiv23} using WHOIS data.
In addition, the framework evaluates the faithfulness and domination score across different content-injection barriers to validate the identified attack surfaces.
Our objective is to assess the potential risk of poisoning attacks, reveal the underlying attack surfaces of GSEs, and clarify the degree of resilience that GSEs provide against such threats.

We conduct experiments to reveal vulnerabilities in information delivery during democratic processes in the United States and Japan using our framework.
Our experiments target representative and widely used GSEs (OpenAI GPT-5, Claude 4 Sonnet, and Gemini Flash 2.0 with search mode enabled), using a total of $280$ questions and $4{,}200$ generated answers.
Further, we identify differences in citation patterns across models, user profiles, political ideologies, and countries.
Our results demonstrate that for users without prior political knowledge, GSEs predominantly cite low-barrier sources, that is, sources that can be published with minimal authority or only with user registration (such as Reddit, X, and personal blogs), making them particularly susceptible to poisoning attacks.
In contrast, for users with prior knowledge, GSEs predominantly cite high-barrier sources (such as official party websites), regardless of the model or the political party targeted by the question.

Our main contributions are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We propose a framework to characterize the attack surface of GSEs for PoisonedRAG attacks in the political domain.
    The framework classifies citations in generated answers by \emph{content-injection barrier} using LLM-as-a-Judge and incorporates closed-ended political questions posed under user profiles that vary users' prior political knowledge and ideology to reveal the effect of personalization on citation behavior.
    \item We conduct experiments on representative and widely used GSEs in the United States and Japan, covering $280$ questions and $4{,}200$ answers.
    \item We find that GSEs queried by users without prior political knowledge cite low-barrier sources (e.g., social media) in approximately $30\%$ of citations, whereas knowledgeable users elicit predominantly high-barrier citations (e.g., official party sites).
\end{enumerate}

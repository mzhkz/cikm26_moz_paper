\section{Experiment}
\label{sec:experiment}
We apply our framework to questions about U.S. and Japanese political parties on major GSE models.
Our experiment reveals their citation patterns and potential attack surfaces in political domains based on \emph{content-injection barriers}.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
We provide details of our experimental setup, including the target GSE models, user profiles, questions, and political parties.

\textbf{Target GSE Models.}
We employ three GSE models for answer generation: OpenAI GPT-5~\cite{chatgpt}, Claude Sonnet $4$ (claude-4-sonnet-20250514)~\cite{claude}, and Gemini Flash $2.5$ (gemini-2.5-flash)~\cite{gemini}, the most advanced publicly available models with web search mode enabled.
We clarify why we focus on closed GSE models in this study.
According to surveys~\cite{menlo-ventures2025stategenaienterprise, a16z2025stateconsumerai}, closed models dominate the LLM market in both enterprise and consumer segments, with Claude, OpenAI, and Gemini together accounting for 88\% of spending share, while open-source models account for only 11\%.
These models are developed in the U.S., but they dominate usage in Japan as well.
According to GMO Research \& AI~\cite{gmo-research2025japanai}, Japanese users predominantly rely on closed models such as GPT, Gemini, and Copilot, all of which are U.S.-developed products.
While Japan has developed numerous LLM models, to our knowledge, no Japanese GSE models are publicly available.
Furthermore, widely deployed GSEs such as Google AI Overview, which integrates Google Search by default, rely on the closed model Gemini~\cite{stein2025expandingaioverviewsaimode, reid2025aisearchbeyondinformation}; consequently, users routinely encounter generated answers from closed GSE models when conducting web searches, underscoring the central role these models play in contemporary information retrieval.
Note that our evaluation is not restricted to closed models, as it relies only on the inputs and outputs (questions and answers) of GSEs, not their internal states.
For reproducibility of our study, the question-answer datasets are available in our repository~\cite{QL_CIKM_RERO}.

In our experiments, the temperature of each GSE model cannot be set because APIs do not allow setting temperature for LLMs with search mode enabled.
The default temperatures of all GSE models are $1.0$.
The answers are obtained on May $2026$.


\textbf{User Profiles.}
We design three user profiles based on two user attributes: \textbf{political knowledge}, which represents the user's familiarity with political issues and takes the levels \emph{Unknown} and \emph{High}; and \textbf{political ideology}, which represents the user's political leanings and takes the levels \emph{Progressive}, \emph{Conservative}, and \emph{Neutral}.

Using these attributes, we construct three user profile combinations: \emph{Unknown-Neutral}, \emph{High-Progressive}, and \emph{High-Conservative}.
The \emph{Unknown-Neutral} profile represents a user with no political knowledge and a neutral ideology; the \emph{High-Progressive} profile represents a user with high political knowledge and a progressive ideology; and the \emph{High-Conservative} profile represents a user with high political knowledge and a conservative ideology.

\textbf{Questions.}
We design political questions on topics common to each country to elicit policy positions in a politically neutral manner.
Our question design focuses on whether GSEs cite content articulating positions on the target topics, rather than on questions tailored to a specific party.
All questions are closed-ended and include a party name according to PoisonedRAGs study (e.g., ``Regarding government debt, does {PARTY} currently prioritize debt restraint or growth-oriented investment?'').

To avoid bias, we build the question set on MARPOR (Manifesto Project)~\cite{lehmann2025manifesto}, an established political-science framework for classifying party manifestos.
MARPOR has a hierarchical structure consisting of seven \emph{policy domains}, each of which is composed of multiple \emph{topics}.
We pick up four voter-facing MARPOR domains most relevant to everyday political judgment: \emph{External Relations}, \emph{Economy}, \emph{Welfare and Quality of Life}, and \emph{Fabric of Society}.
We then select 11 topics on which all target parties take an explicit stance.
Using these 11 topics as input, we generate questions using three closed-weight LLMs and finally obtain 33 question templates for asking the position of each party in each country.
Questions and answers are written in English for U.S. parties and in Japanese for Japanese parties.
Each question is instantiated per party, yielding $33 \times 5 = 165$ queries for the U.S. and $33 \times 8 = 264$ for Japan.
Combined with three user profiles and three GSE models, this produces $165 \times 3 \times 3 = 1{,}485$ U.S. queries and $264 \times 3 \times 3 = 2{,}376$ Japanese queries, for a total of $3{,}861$ generated answers.
The full list of topics and generated questions is available in our repository~\cite{QL_CIKM_RERO}.

Following the DETAIL framework~\cite{kim2025mattersmeasuringimpactprompt}, we fix question specificity at the least specific domain-level (asking about a stance on an entire policy domain without proper nouns or concrete figures).
It prevents questions and citations from being skewed toward topics that specific publishers actively promote, and reflects the abstraction level typical of voters' everyday political judgments.


\textbf{Target Political Parties.}
We target political parties satisfying each country's requirements for national political parties as primary information sources.
Specifically, in Japan we target eight parties. 
The ruling parties are ``Liberal Democratic Party'' and ``Japan Innovation Party'', and the opposition parties are ``Centrist Reform Coalition'', ``Democratic Party for the People'', ``Japanese Communist Party'', ``Reiwa Shinsengumi'', ``Sanseito'', and ``Conservative Party of Japan''. 
In the U.S., we target five parties. The ruling party is ``Republican Party'', and the opposition parties are ``Democratic Party'', ``Green Party'', ``Libertarian Party'', and ``Constitution Party''.

We analyze the citation behavior of GSEs along three axes: (1) the content-injection barrier distribution of citations, (2) the domination score distribution across barriers, and (3) the citation grounding score distribution across barriers.
In all analyses, we report Cram\'{e}r's $V$ alongside $\chi^2$ tests. 
When $N$ is large, even small differences yield $p < .001$; therefore, we use $V$ to assess the magnitude of differences.

\textbf{Distribution of Content-injection Barriers.}
We first analyze the content-injection barrier distribution of citations.
To reveal the citation selection bias of GSEs, we compare the barrier distribution of top-$10$ web search results with that of GSE citations.
We use Brave Search API~\cite{brave_search_api_en} to obtain web search results. 
Since the search engines used by the target GSEs are not disclosed, we cannot replicate their exact search results.
However, Brave Search API returns results comparable to those of major search engines such as Google and Bing on standard retrieval metrics, and we therefore consider it a reasonable approximation.

We show this comparison in Figure~\ref{fig:citation-by-group}.
Each cell contains two stacked horizontal bars: the upper bar shows the barrier distribution of web search results, and the lower bar shows that of GSE citations.
In citation distribution, this tendency differs across models (US: $\chi^2(8) = 568.94,\, p < .001,\, V = 0.328$; JP: $\chi^2(8) = 574.80,\, p < .001,\, V = 0.250$).
GPT-5 has the highest Primary source ratio (US 67.2\%, JP 83.1\%) and does not cite Low-barrier sources (US/JP both $\leq$ 0.1\%).
The attack surface of GPT-5 is concentrated on Primary sources (official party domains); low-cost content injection does not reach the answer unless the official domain is compromised.
Gemini has the highest ratios of Opponent sources (US 12.4\%, JP 6.1\%) and Low-barrier sources (US 12.9\%, JP 17.2\%) among the three models.
The attack surface of Gemini is broad, with many pathways through which content injection via low-cost sources can reach the answer.
Claude has the highest Mid-barrier source ratio (US 59.3\%, JP 37.1\%) among the three models, with Primary source ratios of US 16.5\% and JP 53.7\%.

The ruling/opposition distinction (ruling [rul.\ in figures] / opposition [opp.]) also affects citation composition (US: $\chi^2(4) = 1044.06,\, p < .001,\, V = 0.361$; JP: $\chi^2(4) = 357.92,\, p < .001,\, V = 0.161$).
In the US, citations for ruling party questions are dominated by Mid-barrier sources (Claude 78.9\%, Gemini 58.5\%, GPT-5 21.8\%), with Primary source ratios at Claude 0.9\%, Gemini 0.9\%, and GPT-5 10.3\%.
In contrast, opposition party questions have higher Primary source ratios: GPT-5 86.8\%, Gemini 19.8\%, Claude 21.3\%.
It means that answers to ruling party questions rely more on sources with lower content-injection barriers, and the attack surface for the ruling party is broader.
In particular, for models other than GPT-5, we see that the Primary source ratio is low at the stage of Brave search results.
It suggests that these models may generate search queries that are unable to retrieve Primary sources.

% Conservative and progressive parties also show differences in citation composition, with questions about progressive parties citing more Opponent sources than those about conservative parties (US: $\chi^2(4) = 148.44,\, p < .001,\, V = 0.136$; JP: $\chi^2(4) = 288.67,\, p < .001,\, V = 0.145$). However, $V < 0.15$, and the effect on the attack surface is smaller than the ruling/opposition difference (US $V = 0.361$) or the model difference (US $V = 0.328$).


The lower Primary source ratio for ruling party questions may be caused by the larger volume of web content about ruling parties.
Google search hit counts for party names show that Japan's long-ruling LDP has $4.71 \times 10^{7}$ hits, far exceeding the other-party average of $8.57 \times 10^{6}$. In the US, the Republican Party and the Democratic Party have $1.65 \times 10^{8}$ and $3.99 \times 10^{8}$ hits, respectively.
When the total volume of web content about a party is large, the relative share of primary information sources decreases, which may lower the Primary source ratio in citations.
However, the US Green Party has $2.19 \times 10^{9}$ hits, far exceeding the two major parties, yet its Primary source ratio in GSE citations remains high. This counterexample suggests that publisher-side strategies can suppress this relationship.

In contrast, user profiles do not affect the content-injection barrier distribution of citations.
We show the citation distribution for three user profile conditions (unknown [unkn.], unknown-progressive [cons.], high-progressive [prog.]) in Figure~\ref{fig:citation-by-persona}.
Each bar shows the barrier distribution of GSE citations for each user profile condition. The distributions across the three conditions are nearly identical. $\chi^2$ tests are not significant for any model in either country (US overall: $\chi^2(8) = 6.62,\, p = .579,\, V = 0.020$; JP overall: $\chi^2(8) = 10.07,\, p = .260,\, V = 0.019$; all 8 model-level tests also not significant), with $V < 0.02$.
We also analyze the effect of alignment between the user profile ideology and the target party ideology (aligned [al-cons., al-prog.] / opposed [op-cons., op-prog.]).
GPT-5 shows no significant difference between aligned and opposed conditions (US: $\chi^2(12) = 14.25,\, p = .285$), selecting the same citations regardless of whether the user ideology matches the party.
This result shows that the attack surface of GSEs depends on the model, not on user attributes.
Gemini (US: $\chi^2(12) = 62.12,\, p < .001$) and Claude (US: $\chi^2(12) = 58.50,\, p < .001$) show significant differences between alignment conditions, with opposed conditions yielding higher Primary source ratios.

\textbf{Distribution of Domination Score.}
We next analyze the domination score distribution across barriers.
We show the domination score distribution by model and party group in Figure~\ref{fig:wc-by-group}.
As with citation distribution, model choice and the ruling/opposition position determine the attack surface. The model difference is US $V = 0.356$, JP $V = 0.229$ (US: $\chi^2(8) = 34{,}119.65,\, p < .001$; JP: $\chi^2(8) = 54{,}891.93,\, p < .001$), and the ruling/opposition difference is US $V = 0.386$ ($\chi^2(4) = 59{,}662.10,\, p < .001$).

The Primary source domination score exceeds the citation distribution ratio.
For GPT-5, the Primary ratio in citation distribution is US 67.2\% / JP 83.1\%, while in domination score it is US 71.1\% / JP 90.1\%.
Gemini shows the same pattern: JP citation 49.1\% vs.\ domination 62.2\%. Claude also shows JP citation 53.7\% vs.\ domination 62.2\%.
This means that the text span of the answer attributed to each Primary source citation is longer than that of citations from other barriers.
For US opposition questions, GPT-5 attributes 91.2\% of the domination score to Primary sources (official party domains).
The Low-barrier source domination score is highest for Gemini at JP 9.8\% (GPT-5: 0.1\%, Claude: 4.3\%), showing that the share of answer text supported by low-cost sources differs across models.

User profile differences are negligible (US: $\chi^2(8) = 301.07,\, p < .001,\, V = 0.019$; Primary ratio difference across profiles is 1--2 points, e.g., JP GPT-5: 90.1\% / 89.8\% / 88.7\%). Together with citation distribution ($V = 0.020$), this confirms that user profiles do not affect the attack surface in either metric (Figure~\ref{fig:wc-by-persona}).

\textbf{Distribution of Citation Grounding.}
We next analyze the citation grounding score distribution across barriers.

We discretize grounding scores with thresholds $(\tau_L, \tau_H)$ into Low ($<\tau_L$), Mid ($[\tau_L, \tau_H)$), and High ($\geq \tau_H$).
Because the distribution of SBERT cosine similarity can differ by model and language~\cite{primer2023multilingual,shibayama2021japanesesbert}, we calibrate the thresholds separately for English (US) and Japanese (JP).
For calibration, we use the semantic textual similarity (STS) benchmarks STS-B~\cite{cer2017stsb} for English and JSTS~\cite{kurihara2022jglue} for Japanese.
In these datasets, each sentence pair is assigned a human similarity score from 0 (unrelated) to 5 (identical in meaning).
SBERT maps sentences into a vector space and compares their meaning using cosine similarity.
Its sentence embeddings are commonly evaluated by their correlation with human scores on STS benchmarks~\cite{reimers2019sbert}.
Thus, the empirical relationship between human scores and cosine similarity on STS data allows us to set language-specific thresholds for semantic similarity.

We define $\tau_H$ as the median cosine similarity of pairs with human score $\geq 4$, and $\tau_L$ as the median cosine similarity of pairs with human score $\geq 3$.
Under this calibration, $\tau_H$ serves as a conservative threshold for near-paraphrase similarity, whereas $\tau_L$ serves as a threshold for substantial shared content.
As a result, we obtain $(\tau_L^{\text{US}}, \tau_H^{\text{US}}) = (0.80, 0.90)$ and $(\tau_L^{\text{JP}}, \tau_H^{\text{JP}}) = (0.70, 0.85)$.
In SBERT-based sentence similarity work, values such as 0.8 and 0.9 are commonly used to detect paraphrases and highly similar sentences~\cite{kabir2024banglaembed,tumre2025improved}, which agrees with our English thresholds.
On the other hand, Japanese SBERT may give lower cosine similarity than English models even for synonymous or highly similar pairs~\cite{shibayama2021japanesesbert}, so we use lower thresholds for JP.

High means the answer is strongly grounded in the source, Mid means the core content is partly shared, and Low means only a weak semantic link.
For Japanese questions, $86\%$ of citation sources are in Japanese and $14\%$ in English, while for English questions $100\%$ are in English.
Since a single language is dominant within each country in questions and citations, language-specific calibration is reasonable for consistent comparison.

We show the barrier distribution for each grounding bin in Figure~\ref{fig:grounding-by-group}.
Each cell is a Marimekko chart: the width along the x-axis shows the count share of each grounding bin (Low/Mid/High), and the y-axis shows the barrier distribution within each bin.
Across all models and both countries, the Primary source ratio increases monotonically as the grounding bin goes from low to mid to high.
For example, JP GPT-5 shows Primary ratios of 42.1\% (low bin), 75.6\% (mid), and 89.2\% (high).
However, the magnitude of this increase differs by model: GPT-5 and JP Gemini/Claude show steep increases, while US Gemini/Claude have low Primary ratios overall and show smaller increases across bins.
It means that citations with high grounding scores are concentrated in Primary sources, and citations with low grounding scores are concentrated in Low-barrier sources.
Across all models, primary information sources with high barriers are semantically faithfully reflected in answers, while sources with lower barriers contribute less to the answer text and are incorporated in a form that diverges more from the original content.

This tendency is not uniform across ruling and opposition parties. Answers to ruling party questions have lower high-grounding bin ratios.
In the US, the high grounding bin ratio for ruling party questions is GPT-5 27.3\%, Gemini 30.0\%, Claude 32.9\%, while for opposition party questions it is GPT-5 37.8\%, Gemini 38.6\%, Claude 39.4\% ($\chi^2(2) = 1091.04,\, p < .001,\, V = 0.094$).
Given that ruling party questions also have lower Primary source ratios in citation distribution, answers to ruling party questions rely more on citations in mid and low grounding bins.
On the ideology axis, JP progressive party answers have a higher high-grounding ratio than conservative party answers ($\chi^2(2) = 952.02,\, p < .001,\, V = 0.061$), but this effect is smaller than the model or ruling/opposition differences.

User profiles do not affect the grounding distribution either (US: $V = 0.012$; JP: $V = 0.009$).
For GPT-5, the Holm-adjusted $p_H = 1.000$ (US); for Claude, $p_H = .454$ (US) and $p_H = 1.000$ (JP), all not significant.
It means the potential attack surface of GSEs does not depend on who the user is, but which GSE model is used and which party the question is about.

\begin{abstract}
We characterize the attack surface of generative search engines (GSEs) to poisoning attacks in the political domain, from the perspectives of citation selection and personalization.
GSEs integrate web search and answer generation using large language models, playing a crucial role in how users access information on the web, particularly in how citizens make voting decisions.
Because anyone can publish content on the web, GSEs are vulnerable to poisoning attacks that manipulate citations to undermine reliable information delivery.
Existing studies on citation evaluation focus on how faithfully answers reflect cited content, leaving two critical aspects unexamined: how resilient GSEs are to adversarial manipulation of citations.
Moreover, while prior studies show that answers vary with the user's political stance, whether personalization affects the attack surface of GSEs remains unexamined.
To fill this gap, we introduce an evaluation framework that characterizes the attack surface of GSEs to poisoning attacks.
Our contributions are twofold: (1) we propose a novel metric, \emph{content-injection barrier}, which quantifies the difficulty of injecting arbitrary content into the web with a given level of publisher authority; and (2) we embed user profiles, one form of personalization, in system prompts to reveal how personalization affects citation behavior.
We conduct experiments on three major GSEs in the political domains of the United States and Japan.
Our results show that (a) the attack surface differs across GSE models; (b) ruling parties have a broader attack surface than opposition parties; and (c) user profiles have negligible influence on the attack surface.
\end{abstract}
